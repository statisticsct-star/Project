Dynamic AD–AS: growth benchmark, business cycles, and policy analysis

This engine extends the static AD–AS diagram into a dynamic teaching model. A weak-colour upward benchmark shows potential GDP and steady price-level growth. Realised curves and time-series paths are drawn in stronger colours as the economy moves through resource-boom and global-financial-crisis cycles.

    What the benchmark means
    The benchmark is a moving reference path: potential GDP rises at a constant rate, LRAS shifts right, SRAS shifts right with productive capacity, and AD shifts right enough to support both higher potential output and a steady price-level path. It is drawn weakly so it acts as a reference, not the main realised outcome.
    What business cycles mean here
    A business cycle is a deviation from the moving benchmark. A resource boom creates an upswing when AD rises faster than the benchmark, partly offset by stronger SRAS or LRAS. A GFC-style shock creates a downturn when AD falls below the benchmark and opens a recessionary gap.
    How to use it for policy analysis
    • No discretionary stabilization: shows raw cyclical forces.
    • Output-gap stabilization: shifts AD against the cycle to move GDP back toward LRAS.
    • Inflation-focused stabilization: shifts AD to keep the price level close to its benchmark path.
    • Balanced policy: combines output and price objectives, illustrating AD–AS trade-offs after supply shocks.
    Teaching interpretation
    The dynamic graph is still a teaching model. It is not a full DSGE or empirical forecasting system. Its value is to make clear that macroeconomic policy is often about stabilising deviations from a growing path rather than returning to a fixed static point.
