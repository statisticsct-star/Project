    How monetary policy shifts AD
    A cash-rate cut lowers borrowing costs, debt servicing and expected returns on safe assets. This supports consumption, housing, investment, asset prices and sometimes net exports, so AD shifts right. A cash-rate increase works in the opposite direction and shifts AD left.
    Unconventional and credit-market channels
    Forward guidance, liquidity operations, asset purchases and term funding can support AD when normal transmission is weak. Liquidity and credit-market support can also ease funding-cost pressure, shifting SRAS right/down, and can protect LRAS by preventing financial stress from damaging firms, jobs and productive capacity.
    Policy trade-offs
    • Recession: expansionary monetary policy can close a recessionary gap but may raise the price level.
    • Inflation: contractionary monetary policy cools AD but may reduce GDP and employment.
    • Financial stability: liquidity operations can prevent credit stress from shifting AD left and SRAS left/up.
    • GFC lesson: rate cuts and liquidity support are most powerful when they stop a financial shock from amplifying a collapse in private demand.
    Teaching interpretation
    This is a stylised classroom model, not an empirical forecast. It helps students separate channels: immediate AD support, credit-cost relief through SRAS, expectations, exchange-rate effects, and longer-run effects from avoiding hysteresis.
