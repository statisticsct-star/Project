What the diagram is teaching
  • Efficient provision: for a public good, compare the vertically summed marginal-benefit curve with marginal cost.
  • Free-rider problem: if each person reveals only their own willingness to pay, provision can stop at Qv, below the efficient quantity Q*.
  • Why vertical summation matters: all beneficiaries consume the same quantity, so society adds willingness to pay at each quantity rather than adding quantities at one price.
  • Deadweight loss from under-provision: the shaded region between Qv and Q* captures forgone net benefits when socially valuable units are not provided.

Interpretation note
This case is a stylized public-goods translation of Australia’s Flood Warning Infrastructure Network program. The observed data tell us that flood-warning infrastructure was fragmented across many owners, while benefits spilled to households, emergency services, farms, businesses and governments. The diagram turns that coordination problem into a clean teaching sequence about vertical summation, efficient provision, and why higher-level public finance can reduce free-riding.

Named sources used for the case: Bureau of Meteorology, Flood warning network upgrades; Australian Government joint media releases on 15 May 2023 and 24 September 2024 about the National Flood Warning Infrastructure Network and the Queensland rollout.
