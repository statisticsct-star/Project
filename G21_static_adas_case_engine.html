How to interpret the static AD–AS model
  • Demand shocks: a left shift in AD lowers output and the price level; a right shift raises both.
  • Supply shocks: an adverse SRAS shift lowers output and raises prices, producing stagflation; a favourable SRAS shift raises output and lowers prices.
  • Inflationary and recessionary gaps: compare short-run equilibrium output with LRAS. A positive gap means output is above potential; a negative gap means unused capacity and weaker labour demand.
  • Stabilization: fiscal and monetary policy mainly shift AD. That makes them powerful against demand shocks, but only imperfect against adverse supply shocks because closing the output gap can worsen inflation.

Pre-pandemic inflation Annual CPI was 1.8% in Dec 2019 Anchors the baseline near low inflation and roughly normal activity before the pandemic shock.
Pandemic recession GDP fell 7.0% q/q in Jun 2020 and unemployment reached 7.5% in Jul 2020 Mapped into a large leftward AD shock that creates a recessionary gap and lower prices.
Fiscal support Treasury reported $259 billion of fiscal and balance-sheet support, around 13.3% of annual GDP, with direct fiscal measures around 6.9% of GDP Used as the real-world anchor for the expansionary fiscal component that shifts AD back toward potential.
Monetary support and later tightening Cash rate 0.75% in Feb 2020, 0.10% by Nov 2020, and 4.35% by Nov 2023 Used to motivate expansionary monetary policy in the recession step and contractionary monetary stabilization in the disinflation step.
Rebound and later inflation GDP rose 3.4% q/q in Dec 2021 and was 3.4% above Dec 2019; annual CPI then peaked at 7.8% in Dec 2022 Motivates the move from a closed gap to a high-price equilibrium shaped by both stronger demand and adverse supply conditions.
Disinflation Annual CPI fell to 2.4% in Dec 2024 while unemployment was 4.0% Anchors the final step in which tighter policy cools demand and pushes inflation back toward target.
Interpretation note
This is best read as a stylized macro teaching sequence. The official Australian data tell us that the economy moved from a severe recessionary gap in 2020, to a stimulus-supported recovery, to a high-inflation period in 2022–23, and then to disinflation by late 2024. The static AD–AS diagram turns that evidence into a clean classroom story about shocks, gaps, and the strengths and limits of stabilization policy.
