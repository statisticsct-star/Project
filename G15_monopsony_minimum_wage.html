G15. Monopsony, labor-market power, and minimum wage under monopsony

Compare the monopsony benchmark with the competitive benchmark, then add a minimum wage and see when it raises wages alone, raises both wages and employment, or becomes too high and cuts hiring. The case study uses the 1992 New Jersey fast-food minimum-wage episode as a real-world teaching calibration.

Decision rules
Monopsony: choose employment where MFC = MRP, then read the wage from the labor-supply curve.
Competition: employment is where labor supply = MRP.
Minimum wage: if the wage floor is between the monopsony wage and the competitive wage, the firm hires more workers because the effective marginal cost of labor becomes flatter up to the kink.

Case interpretation
  • The graph is a stylized monopsony teaching translation of the New Jersey fast-food episode, not a structural estimate of the exact labor-supply and MRP curves.
  • The supporting charts use the paper’s reported before-and-after means for starting wages and FTE employment in New Jersey and eastern Pennsylvania.
  • A later labor-market-concentration literature gives the monopsony interpretation extra support by showing that minimum-wage employment effects are more positive in more concentrated low-wage labor markets.
