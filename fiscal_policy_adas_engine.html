    How fiscal policy shifts AD
    Government purchases directly add to aggregate demand. Transfers and tax cuts raise disposable income and can shift AD right through consumption. Spending cuts and tax rises shift AD left. These are non-price determinants because they change planned spending at any given price level.
    Balanced-budget policy
    When government spending and taxes rise together, the direct spending effect can exceed the tax-induced fall in private consumption, so AD may still shift right. If the spending is productive public investment, SRAS and LRAS can also shift right over time. A balanced-budget contraction works in the opposite direction.
    Policy trade-offs
    • Recession: expansionary fiscal policy can close a recessionary gap but may raise the price level.
    • Inflation: contractionary fiscal policy cools AD but may reduce GDP and employment.
    • Supply design: infrastructure and productivity-enhancing spending can make fiscal policy affect LRAS as well as AD.
    • GFC lesson: fiscal stimulus is often used when private demand contracts sharply and monetary policy alone may not restore confidence quickly.
    Teaching interpretation
    This is a stylised classroom model, not an empirical forecast. It helps students separate channels: immediate AD support, short-run supply pressure or relief, and longer-run potential-output effects.
