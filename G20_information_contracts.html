What the lenses teach
  • Adverse selection: when households know more about their own risk than insurers, pooled full insurance can attract too many high-risk applicants.
  • Screening: a separating contract usually keeps some deductible or excess so high-risk households do not want to imitate low-risk households.
  • Signalling: verified information such as building-hardening evidence can move a household from the pooled line to the low-risk line.
  • Principal-agent: after the policy is written, full insurance can weaken care incentives. The second-best contract often restores some skin in the game.
  • Incentive compatibility: the green IC guide marks contracts that satisfy the relevant no-mimic or high-effort condition.

Interpretation note
This case uses north Australian home insurance because it brings all five concepts together in one setting. Risky households and safer, better-hardened households are hard to distinguish; insurers use pricing and excesses to screen; households can signal resilience through verified upgrades; and some post-contract actions, such as maintenance or storm preparation, remain partially hidden.
