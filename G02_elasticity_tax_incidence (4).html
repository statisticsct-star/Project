Elasticity, tax incidence, pass-through, and responsiveness to shocks

Graph G02 rotates demand and supply around a fixed benchmark equilibrium so students can isolate how elasticity changes the split of a tax or cost shock, the pass-through into price, and the responsiveness of quantity. The case-study tab then connects the same logic to the OPEC oil market over 1970–1985, using annual price and production data.

Drag the blue demand handle or red supply handle vertically to rotate the curves around the benchmark equilibrium. At the common benchmark equilibrium E₀, a flatter line implies a larger elasticity and a steeper line implies a smaller elasticity. Along any straight-line demand curve, however, elasticity still changes from elastic in the upper region to inelastic in the lower region, with unit elasticity at the midpoint. Light-gray benchmark curves are shown only when they differ from the current curves, so the benchmark case stays uncluttered.
How to read this graph
Tax incidence In a tax case, the orange buyer block and purple seller block show how the burden is split. The more inelastic side bears more.
Pass-through Buyer pass-through equals the fraction of the cost shock that shows up in the buyer price.
Quantity response At a common benchmark equilibrium, flatter curves allow larger quantity adjustment. Steeper curves push more of the adjustment into price.
Linear-demand reminder On a straight-line demand curve, the upper part is elastic, the midpoint is unit elastic, and the lower part is inelastic. This graph compares point elasticities at E₀.
